Odes & Songs

Selected odes, songs, chants, epitaphs, and mysterious fragments from The Saga of Black Wings, where the world often speaks in rhythm before it speaks in prose.

The Voice of the Saga

In the Black Wings world, odes and songs are not decoration. They carry memory, grief, warnings, old law, humour, love, battle, prophecy, animal witness, and the truths that ordinary speech cannot always hold.

Some pieces appear inside the books. Others are archive fragments, road-songs, hearth-songs, Druid chants, or spoiler-safe glimpses of the wider saga.

A song may remember what a kingdom tried to bury.

Opening Archive Fragment

A spoiler-safe invocation for new readers entering the Black Wings world.

Ode of the Black Wings Road
Archive Fragment
No road began clean. Mud knew it first. Then feather. Then foot. Then blood, though blood is always late to admit what the earth has already written. The raven flew low over fields that had forgotten how many names lay under them. The crow watched from a broken fence and judged the living with excellent patience. A child stepped where no map had given permission. A hound sneezed at danger. A wolf waited where lies hoped to pass. A woman lit the hearth without asking ownership to bless the flame. A man carried an axe that knew the difference between severing harm and cutting what still needed care. So the road opened. Not because it was safe. Not because it was kind. Because somewhere ahead, a locked thing had mistaken silence for victory. And the Black Wings had never been fond of that particular mistake.

Hearth-Song

A short hearth-piece for the warmth, food, jokes, animals, and chosen-family side of the saga.

Song of the Bowl Passed Twice
Hearth-Song
Pass the bowl once for hunger. Pass it twice for pride. Some mouths have learned to call need weakness. Some hands have learned to hide until shaking becomes custom. So pass the bowl once and do not stare. Pass it twice and tell a joke. Let Hugo lean on the wrong boot. Let Blue insult the spoon. Let Benson sit where no one asked him to sit and improve the meeting by force of body. Let Scarlet watch the window. Let Mad sneeze at whatever old trouble thinks itself hidden in the rafters. Let Ness warm the broth without making gratitude stand at attention. Let FATAS grumble and fetch more bread. That is how a hearth learns not to become a throne. That is how care keeps its hands clean. Not by being small. By asking. By offering. By staying. By passing the bowl twice.

Druid Chant

A chant of old magic, beast-law, scent, reed, paw, flame, mist, and consent.

Druid Chant of Reed, Paw, Flame, and Scent
Old Marsh Chant
Reed bends. Mud keeps. Paw knows. Claw waits. Feather sees where eye refuses. Fur hears what pride has buried. Flame lowers. Mist gathers. Scent walks only where choice opens. Name the road, but do not own it. Touch the gate, but do not force it. Call the beast, but do not command it. Ask the child, then wait. Ask the wound, then wait. Ask the door, then wait. Old magic remembers what old power forgot: Nothing living belongs to the hand that can reach it.

Book XIV Hint Plate

A spoiler-safe hint toward The House Beyond the House.

Ode of the Door That Opened Too Soon
From the Northern Road
The door opened before the hand had lifted. That was the warning. Warm light spilled out. Soup steamed. Blankets waited. A chair sat near the fire with its arms spread wide as if comfort itself had carved the wood. But Jack growled. Jórunn covered her nose. Blue said something deeply unhelpful and entirely correct. The leopard was not visible, which meant everyone wise made room for it. Ness looked at the open door and saw the price standing behind the welcome. FATAS looked at the warm room and remembered a colder one. Mia heard laughter where laughter had no right to be trusted yet. Maisie signed: OPEN IS NOT PROOF. James answered: ASKING IS THE PROOF. So no one entered. Not yet. For the Braid had learned what many houses fear the living will discover: A door may open wide and still be another lock with better manners.

Animal Verse

A lighter piece, because the animals are often the first to know and the last to behave.

Blue’s Complaint Before Breakfast
Possibly Authorised by Blue, Possibly Not
I warned you. I warned you in feather. I warned you in twig. I warned you by stealing the important ribbon and dropping it in the lap of the only woman with sense enough to suspect a ribbon. Did anyone listen? No. Humans require evidence, minutes, committee feeling, and three dramatic silences before admitting a bird may have been correct. I was correct before breakfast. This is difficult. Greatness often is. Now pass the crust. Not that crust. The better one. Honestly, how any of you survived before my arrival remains a question for the darker scholars.

Love-Ode Fragment

A spoiler-safe love fragment for the heart of FATAS and Ness.

Fragment for the One Who Stood Beside
Love-Ode Fragment
She did not stand before him like rescue. She did not stand behind him like shadow. She stood beside. That ruined every old law that required love to kneel, own, obey, or disappear. Beside is a dangerous word to crowns. Beside is a door blood cannot lock. Beside is a hearth that warms without ruling. Beside is the hand that takes your hand and still leaves it yours. So when the road darkened, and the house remembered, and the old wound opened one more eye, she stood beside him. And the dark, which had prepared for fear, found itself facing love with its boots on.

Epitaphs and Laws

The saga often opens or closes acts with epitaphs, laws, fragments, or lines that feel carved into wood, ash, stone, feather, or memory.

No road is walked alone.
No child is a key before being fed.
Origin is not ownership.
Need is not consent.
Mercy is not ransom.
Open is not proof.
Asking is the proof.
A helper names nothing without consent.
The beast that waits may know more than the king who speaks.
A song may carry the truth until a mouth is ready.

From the Cold Hearth

A short extract-style plate connected to the northern danger of Book XIV.

Fragment of the Hearths That Burned Cold
Skeld-Fell Fragment
In Skeld-Fell, the fires did not go out. That was the cruelty. Had darkness come plainly, had ash settled where flame once danced, the clan might have known what enemy stood before them. But the fires burned. Blue-grey. Low. Beautiful in the way a frozen knife is beautiful under moonlight. They burned. They gave no warmth. A child asked why the fire hated them. No one answered. And when need grew teeth, the old stories were opened. Not the whole stories. Never the whole stories. Only the useful bones.

Why the Odes Matter

The odes are part of the saga’s architecture. They give the books a second voice: half archive, half song, half warning, which is too many halves, but the ravens have never cared for tidy counting.

They let the world remember itself. They let readers feel the law before the plot explains it. They make space for grief, humour, prophecy, beauty, anger, and wonder to stand together without asking which one owns the room.

The prose walks the road. The odes teach the road how to echo.
